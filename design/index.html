Spark Business for Capital One

Sign-in and first time experience.

The Spark Business web and mobile app lets small business owners open and manage a business checking account. When I joined the design team, the Spark Business pilot had been running for a year without clear performance metrics.

I worked with researchers, designers, developers and stakeholders throughout Capital One to dig into the effectiveness of the pilot. We reviewed analytics data, spoke to customers and ran usability tests. This information revealed the ideal path for small business customers to open an account and make their first deposit.

Design Research

After a thorough review of existing user research reports, I worked with the analytics and customer experience teams to understand the data we had collected during the pilot.

I found low conversion rates when potential customers abandoned the account opening process, especially during long wait times. The customer support team confirmed that almost half of all support calls were regarding document verification or account status during enrollment. We looked deeper into this problem during in-person and remote usability sessions.

Usability Studies

Comparing analytics and customer support data with remote usability findings showed that we were barely getting half of out customers through the account opening process. I partnered with a researcher in Virginia and we ran two in-person studies to learn more about the tone and expectation so we could improve the experience and build trust with our customers.

User Experience Design

Working alongside developers in the Spark Business web and mobile product teams, I created wireframes for weekly releases, as well as a proposal for an overhaul to the authentication system. To update authentication, I worked with Capital One consumer product teams for Wallet, Money and SwiftID. The feedback I collected helped us build a consistent content strategy and user experience for Spark Business on web, Android and iPhone.

Development and Production

During development, I updated assets and answered questions from engineers and product managers.

HR Portal for Kaiser Permanente

Intranet redesign and style guide.

The Human Resources portal for Kaiser Permanente serves over 160,000 employees across six United States regions and is the most-used internal website with 40,000 transactions per day. It was created ten years earlier and had become a burden for employees.

Employees must visit the portal to view paychecks, do compliance training and update health benefits. The challenge was to redesign the website in a way that made resources easier to use and easier to update for our partners in Payroll, Compliance and Benefits.

Design Research

Building on years of research before I joined the team, I worked with a design researcher to create a set of usability heuristics so we could measure our findings from remote and in-person research sessions.

We mapped the information architecture for the portal overall, then created journey maps for specific subject areas like benefits, payroll and employee satisfaction surveys. As part of the design research team, I visited employees in company cafeterias, sat down with hospital staff in their offices and ran remote sessions to learn how they used the system.

These usability sessions showed that findability was key. During compliance training, employees frequently clicked a banner ad linked to a compliance activity instead of searching or browsing on the portal. My team knew we could make things better.

Style Guide

I built a style guide and managed an intern to create design patterns based on visual design and feedback front-end developers. We created HTML and CSS for navigation, news articles and discussions to visualize website components for developers and content strategists. The guide also included icons, accessibility color standards and a recommendations for applying layouts.

User Experience Design

During weekly design sessions, I documented a redesign strategy using the current and future information architecture. My team used this strategy to guide where and when we implemented each wireframe. These wireframes showed a simplified navigation and clear interactions on web and mobile.

Visual Design

Like our wireframes, I created a simplified visual design. By confining the Kaiser Permanente brand palette to six colors in the style guide, our website easily passed ADA WCAG AA standards.

Development and Production

My team released portal updates quarterly, in synch with our benefits software vendor. I coached my colleagues on agile development practices and the basics of product management to give them a holistic view of the work ahead of us.

Firefly for Kaiser Permanente

Real-time location services pilot.

The CIO Chief of Staff approached my team to create emergency department real-time location services pilot for iPad and iPhone. We helped department staff locate one another to increase the number of doctor and nurse meetings at the patient bedside over a 5-week pilot period. We worked with business strategy, infrastructure teams, clinical directors, doctors and nurses on the ground.

Design Research

I planned and coordinated process-mapping and prioritization exercises for emergency department staff: doctors, nurses and technicians. I combined this knowledge with interviews and observations that we did to help the team learn what was most important to build into our app.

User Experience Design

In addition to speaking with doctors in-person, I sketched interface proposals in pencil and recruited other doctors and nurses to give feedback over video conference. I created high-fidelity wireframes for a role-playing session where I lead physician executives through an emergency department scenario using the app, so that they could imagine how it would be used during the pilot.

Visual Design

Working in partnership with a visual designer, I annotated and guided the creation of a compelling and inviting visual design. We aimed to attract curiosity and convey warmth to the hospital staff.

Prototyping

Beyond role-playing, I collaborated with my team's lead engineer, product manager and tester to build a working prototype of the mobile app in Kaiser Permanente's Innovation Center. We tested sensor boundaries and latency on our mobile app to ensure that locating people and equipment on a map felt natural. Two months of prototype feedback gave us the confidence to move to the pilot stage.

Pilot Program

Connecting to the emergency department sensor network took time. After struggling with deadlines, we piloted the app for 5 weeks, encouraging nurse managers to share the technology with their staff.

Adoption of the pilot app was lower than expected. I managed researchers hired outside Kaiser Permanente to check our hypothesis that emergency department staff wanted to find each other and equipment. We found our hypothesis did not show the whole picture. During the pilot, staff adjusted their needs if they couldn't find someone or something. They looked for equipment and people as a last resort. Interviews after the pilot confirmed that nurses and technicians would prefer to augment their current voice communication system instead of using a mobile app.

Our pilot was successful as a proof-of-technology. Sensors for real-time location services were already installed in many Kaiser Permanente hospitals and my team contributed infrastructure knowledge, reusable development components and documentation for teams to build upon.

Bonfire School: Health, technology and design

How do we stay healthy?

Bonfire School is an organization and community built around design and health. We bring together people of any background to create what they want – crafts, software, dance and more – in a place that supports personal health. Our dream is that it can become a physical school one day.

The project began as a series of stories about community.

Currently, I'm researching wellbeing across cultures to understand how restorative practices – the things we do as humans to regain equilibrium when we fall off-balance – are passed on to future generations.

Public Classes

These classes aim to make learning with an software industry professional fun and engaging. Through a series of group exercises student are prompted to think about the needs of others and how we can solve their problems with software.

There are three main concepts in this series, each with it’s own class.
Learn to find human needs for software
Design software based on human needs
Build software with Python using design

In this series, student learn to listen and spend time with others to know what they need; we design software based on those needs and draw pictures to tell the story of what we can build; then we build software with Python and change it until it meets the needs we've identified.

Graphic Design

Typography and print projects

My design work is heavily inspired by Emigre and Jan Tschichold. As a typesetter in my youth, I studied the history and craft of typography while working at a letterpress print shop. Everything I do is grounded in the principles of modern graphic design, grid systems and balance.

Letterpress Print Work

I produced trade show flyers for a home performance contracting firm. Potential customers could pick up the flyers and feel blind-embossed card stock printed in earth tones. It was meant to evoke a connection to health and comfort in the home.

Print and Digital Work

I created album art and flyers for Overlap, where I reflected the joy of discovery and creation with others. As a member of Overlap, a worldwide media arts community based in San Francisco, I contributed graphic design, wrote about the community and hosted events with other members.
